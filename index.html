01Body Organization & Directional Terms

Directional terms describe relative position — always evaluated in anatomical position (standing, facing forward, palms out).

🧭 Remember
Pairs: anterior/posterior · superior/inferior · medial/lateral · proximal/distal · superficial/deep
Fill in the blank

The shoulder is _______ to the elbow, and the elbow is _______ to the shoulder.

The shoulder is proximal to the elbow; the elbow is distal to the shoulder. Proximal = closer to the point of attachment; distal = farther away.
True / False + Explain

True or False: The kneecap is superior to the hip. Explain your reasoning.

False. Superior means closer to the head. The hip is located above the kneecap, making the hip superior — and the kneecap inferior — to the hip.
Scenario

A surgeon notes that the liver is lateral to the stomach, and the stomach is lateral to the spine. Is this consistent? Explain medial vs. lateral.

Yes, this is consistent and illustrates that lateral/medial are relative. Medial = closer to the midline; lateral = farther from the midline. The spine is most medial, the stomach is lateral to the spine, and the liver is lateral to the stomach.
Positioning

A patient is placed face-down on an operating table. Name that position. If they roll over, what is the new position?

Face-down = prone. Face-up = supine. Memory trick: supine — your spine touches the surface below you.
Levels of organization

List the six levels of structural organization from simplest to most complex.

Chemical → Cellular → Tissue → Organ → Organ System → Organism. At each level, structures acquire new properties their parts alone don't possess.
Cavities

What two major body cavities does the diaphragm separate? What organ systems are contained in each?

The diaphragm separates the thoracic cavity (heart, lungs, esophagus) above from the abdominopelvic cavity (stomach, intestines, liver, kidneys, bladder, reproductive organs) below.
Core principle

In anatomy and physiology, what does "structure determines function" mean? Provide an example.

A part can only perform functions that its physical form allows. Example: the biconcave shape of red blood cells maximizes surface area for oxygen binding by hemoglobin. Structure and function are inseparable in biology.
Apply the terms

The nose is _______ and _______ to the ears. (Use two different directional terms.)

The nose is medial (closer to the midline) and anterior (frontal surface) to the ears. Other acceptable: the nose is also inferior to the forehead and superior to the mouth.
02Cell Biology & Membrane Transport

Understand how cells maintain homeostasis and how substances move across cell membranes.

Define

What is homeostasis and why is it considered the primary job of every cell?

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment (temperature, pH, ion concentrations, blood sugar, etc.) within narrow limits. Every cell contributes because biochemical reactions essential to life can only function within those specific conditions.
Compare

What is the difference between diffusion and active transport? Which requires energy and why?

Diffusion is passive — molecules move from high to low concentration with no energy required. Active transport moves molecules against the concentration gradient (low → high), requiring ATP to power carrier proteins because this is thermodynamically unfavorable.
Application

A cell is placed in a hypotonic solution. What happens and why?

The cell swells (and may lyse). A hypotonic solution has a lower solute concentration than the cell interior. Water moves by osmosis from the less concentrated solution into the more concentrated cell interior, increasing cell volume until it bursts if the membrane cannot stretch enough.
Short answer

Sodium ions are moved across a membrane against their concentration gradient. What process is this, and what does the cell spend to accomplish it?

This is active transport. The cell spends ATP. The Na⁺/K⁺ pump is the classic example — it pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per ATP molecule used, maintaining the electrochemical gradient essential for nerve and muscle function.
Process

When vesicles form at the cell membrane to bring in a large particle, what is this process called? How does it differ from diffusion?

Endocytosis (specifically phagocytosis for large particles). Unlike diffusion, endocytosis requires energy (ATP), can move large particles that cannot cross the membrane directly, and involves membrane shape changes to engulf material in a vesicle.
Critical thinking

If a drug blocks all sodium channels in a nerve cell, what would happen to the nerve's ability to transmit signals?

Transmission would stop completely. Sodium channels are essential for depolarization — the first step of an action potential. Without Na⁺ influx, the membrane can never reach threshold and no action potential will fire. This is exactly how local anesthetics (like lidocaine) work.
03The Four Tissue Types

Identify tissues visually and by description; know each type's function and subtypes.

Identify by description

A microscope slide shows large round cells with a thin membrane surrounding what appears to be empty space (the fat was dissolved during preparation). What tissue is this and what are its functions?

Adipose tissue (connective tissue). Functions: energy storage (triglycerides), thermal insulation, physical cushioning/protection of organs. The large "empty" spaces are where lipid droplets were before processing.
Identify by description

A slide shows many small biconcave pink cells with no nucleus, floating in a pale liquid background, with an occasional large cell with a purple-stained lobed nucleus. What tissue type is this?

Blood — a specialized liquid connective tissue. The small cells are erythrocytes (RBCs), the pale background is plasma, and the larger cell with the purple nucleus is a leukocyte (WBC).

Connection

What structure connects epithelial tissue to the connective tissue beneath it, and what is it made of?

A basement membrane — a thin, noncellular layer of glycoproteins and collagen secreted by both epithelial and connective tissue cells. It anchors epithelium, acts as a selective filter, and provides structural support.
Compare

What is the difference between dense regular and dense irregular connective tissue? Where is each found?

Dense regular: collagen fibers all parallel → maximum strength in one direction. Location: tendons (bone to muscle) and ligaments (bone to bone). Dense irregular: collagen fibers in multiple directions → resists tension from all directions. Location: dermis of skin, organ capsules.
Special tissue

What tissue is uniquely designed to stretch dramatically as an organ fills and recoil when it empties? Where is it found?

Transitional epithelium (urothelium). Found in: urinary bladder, ureters, and part of the urethra. Cells can flatten into a single layer when stretched or pile up into multiple dome-shaped layers when the organ is empty.
Nervous tissue

What are the two main cell types in nervous tissue, and what does each do?

Neurons: generate and transmit electrical signals (action potentials). Neuroglia (glial cells): support, protect, insulate, and nourish neurons. Glial cells outnumber neurons roughly 10:1 but don't conduct impulses themselves.
04Integumentary System

Skin layers, accessory structures (glands, hair, nails), and the system's protective functions.

📌 Layer Mnemonic (deep → superficial)
Bad Skin Gives Little Comfort = Basale · Spinosum · Granulosum · Lucidum · Corneum
Sequence

A keratinocyte is born in the deepest epidermal layer. Trace its journey upward, naming each layer and what happens at each stage.

  1. Stratum Basale — produced by mitosis; columnar cells attached to basement membrane.
  2. Stratum Spinosum — pushed upward; spiny projections (desmosomes) provide strength; keratin production begins.
  3. Stratum Granulosum — cells flatten; waterproofing lipids released; nucleus and organelles begin to break down.
  4. Stratum Lucidum — only in thick skin (palms/soles); clear, densely packed dead cells.
  5. Stratum Corneum — outermost; 20–30 layers of dead, fully keratinized cells; shed continuously (desquamation).

Structure & function

When you're cold, you get goosebumps. What structure causes this, where is it located, and what is its purpose?

The arrector pili muscle — a tiny smooth muscle running from the dermis to each hair follicle. When it contracts, it pulls the follicle upright, creating a tiny bump (goosebump). Purpose: in fur-covered animals it traps a warm air layer. In humans it is mostly vestigial.
Nail anatomy

A patient loses the tip of their fingernail in an accident. Will the nail grow back? Where exactly does nail production occur?

Yes — as long as the nail root (germinal matrix beneath the skin at the nail's base) is intact, the nail will regrow. The visible nail body and free edge are just densely packed keratinized dead cells. Only root destruction causes permanent loss.

Burns classification

A first-degree burn affects only the epidermis; a third-degree burn destroys both the epidermis and dermis. Why is a third-degree burn so much more serious?

Destroying the dermis eliminates: blood vessels (causing fluid/protein loss), nerve endings (explaining why 3rd degree burns are often painless — sensors are gone), hair follicles, and sweat glands. Without the dermis, regeneration cannot occur from below and skin grafting is needed. Infection risk also skyrockets without the barrier.

Bone anatomy, fracture types and repair, bone classification, skull and facial bones, sutures, movements, and body landmarks.

Fracture repair sequence

A gymnast breaks her radius. Walk through all four stages of bone healing in the correct order.

  1. Hematoma formation — blood vessels rupture; clot forms; inflammatory chemicals attract repair cells.
  2. Fibrocartilage callus — fibroblasts/chondroblasts invade; soft bridge of fibrocartilage spans the break.
  3. Bony callus ossification — osteoblasts replace fibrocartilage with spongy bone; hard callus forms.
  4. Bone remodeling — osteoclasts remove excess bone; compact bone replaces spongy; original shape restored over months to years.
Classify bones

Classify each: femur, carpal bones, sternum, vertebra, phalanges, skull parietal plates, patella.

Femur → Long. Carpals → Short. Sternum → Flat. Vertebra → Irregular. Phalanges → Long (miniature). Parietal plates → Flat. Patella → Sesamoid (a special type embedded in tendon; sometimes classified as short or irregular).
Cranial and facial bones

Name the four major cranial bones and their locations. Then identify where each skull suture connects bones.

Bones: Frontal (forehead), Parietal ×2 (top/sides), Temporal ×2 (sides/ears), Occipital (back/base).

Sutures: Coronal (frontal ↔ parietals), Sagittal (parietal ↔ parietal, midline), Lambdoidal (parietals ↔ occipital), Squamous (parietals ↔ temporals).
Facial bones

Identify the mandible, maxilla, zygomatic, and nasal bones. Which is the only movable bone of the skull?

Mandible (lower jaw) — only movable skull bone. Maxilla (upper jaw/hard palate). Zygomatic (cheekbone). Nasal (bridge of nose — paired). The mandible's mobility allows chewing and speech.

Fracture types

Describe the visual and mechanical characteristics of a greenstick fracture, comminuted fracture, spiral fracture, and compound (open) fracture.

Greenstick: bone bends and partially cracks on one side — like a green twig. Common in children whose bones are more flexible. Comminuted: bone shatters into three or more fragments. Spiral: fracture line spirals along the bone shaft from twisting/torsional force. Compound/Open: bone fragment pierces through the skin — high infection risk.
Bone identification

Where are the following bones in the body: femur, patella, calcaneus, humerus, carpals, tarsals, metacarpals, metatarsals?

Femur → thigh (longest/strongest bone). Patella → kneecap. Calcaneus → heel. Humerus → upper arm. Carpals → wrist (8 bones). Tarsals → ankle/hindfoot (7 bones). Metacarpals → palm. Metatarsals → foot (similar position to metacarpals but in the foot).
06Muscular System

Muscle organization, sliding filament theory, neuromuscular physiology, and muscle fatigue.

Organization hierarchy

Describe the structural organization of skeletal muscle from the whole muscle down to the individual protein filaments.

Whole muscle (wrapped in epimysium) → Fascicles (wrapped in perimysium) → Muscle fibers/cells (wrapped in endomysium) → Myofibrils → Sarcomeres (functional units) → Actin and myosin filaments.
Sliding filament theory

Step-by-step: how does a muscle fiber contract from the nerve signal to the shortening of the sarcomere?

  1. Nerve impulse reaches the neuromuscular junction; ACh is released into the synaptic cleft.
  2. ACh binds receptors on the muscle cell membrane → action potential generated.
  3. Action potential travels down T-tubules → Ca²⁺ released from sarcoplasmic reticulum.
  4. Ca²⁺ binds troponin → shifts tropomyosin off actin binding sites.
  5. Myosin heads bind actin → cross-bridges form.
  6. Power stroke: myosin heads pivot, pulling actin filaments toward center → sarcomere shortens.
  7. ATP binds myosin head → detachment. Cycle repeats while Ca²⁺ and ATP are available.
Muscle fatigue

Why does muscle fatigue occur after prolonged or intense exercise? Name all contributing factors.

Three simultaneous factors: 1) ATP depletion — no fuel for myosin detachment or cross-bridge cycling. 2) Lactic acid buildup — anaerobic respiration during intense exercise drops cellular pH, impairing enzyme function. 3) ACh depletion — neurotransmitter at the NMJ runs low, weakening the nerve signal to the muscle.
Regulatory proteins

What are the roles of troponin and tropomyosin in muscle contraction? How does calcium "unlock" contraction?

At rest, tropomyosin physically blocks myosin-binding sites on actin, preventing cross-bridge formation. Troponin holds tropomyosin in position. When Ca²⁺ binds troponin, troponin changes shape → pulls tropomyosin aside → binding sites exposed → contraction begins. Removing Ca²⁺ reverses this, causing relaxation.
Functions

Skeletal muscle produces movement — but name two other important whole-body functions it serves.

Maintaining posture: constant low-level contractions of postural muscles (especially along the spine) keep the body upright against gravity. Thermogenesis (heat production): muscle contraction releases heat as a byproduct — this is the main reason we shiver when cold.
Involuntary control

Which muscle tissue types are controlled involuntarily, and where is each found?

Cardiac muscle: involuntary; found only in the heart wall. Smooth muscle: involuntary; found in walls of hollow organs (stomach, intestines, bladder, blood vessels, airways). Skeletal muscle is voluntary. Note: the diaphragm is skeletal but can be partially overridden involuntarily by brainstem control.
07Nervous System

Action potentials, brain lobes, special brain regions, and vision.

⚡ Action Potential Sequence
Resting potential (−70 mV, Na⁺/K⁺ pump active) → Depolarization (Na⁺ floods in) → Repolarization (K⁺ flows out) → Refractory period (pump restores ion balance)
Resting potential

At resting potential, what maintains the −70 mV charge inside the neuron? What happens if this pump is blocked?

The Na⁺/K⁺ pump (using ATP) continuously moves 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in, maintaining a net negative interior. If the pump is blocked: ion gradients dissipate, resting potential is lost, the neuron cannot repolarize, and repeated action potential firing ceases — essentially silencing the nerve.
Depolarization

During depolarization, which ion and which type of channel are involved? In which direction does the ion move?

Voltage-gated sodium (Na⁺) channels open. Na⁺ rushes into the cell, driven by both its concentration gradient (high outside) and the electrical gradient (negative interior attracts positive ions). This makes the interior positive — depolarization.
Refractory period

What specific role does the Na⁺/K⁺ pump play during the refractory period, and why does this period matter for how nerves function?

During the refractory period the Na⁺/K⁺ pump actively restores resting ion concentrations using ATP. This matters for two reasons: 1) it allows the neuron to fire again, and 2) it ensures action potentials travel in only one direction — the region just behind a wave of depolarization cannot immediately fire again.
Brain lobes

Match: which brain lobe handles vision? Which controls voluntary movement and higher reasoning? Which processes hearing and language comprehension?

Vision → Occipital lobe (posterior). Voluntary movement + higher reasoning → Frontal lobe (anterior). Hearing + language comprehension → Temporal lobe (sides). Sensory integration (touch, pressure) → Parietal lobe (top/posterior).
Language areas

Patient A can understand everything you say but cannot produce fluent speech. Patient B speaks fluently but their words make no sense. Which brain area is damaged in each?

Patient A: Broca's area (frontal lobe) — Broca's aphasia; understanding is intact, production is broken. Patient B: Wernicke's area (temporal lobe) — Wernicke's aphasia; they produce "word salad" because comprehension is lost.
Limbic system

A traumatic brain injury patient shows no fear response and is unable to form emotional memories. What structure was likely damaged, and what system does it belong to?

The amygdala, part of the limbic system. It processes emotions — especially fear and aggression — and assigns emotional significance to memories. Bilateral amygdala damage produces the condition seen in Urbach-Wiethe disease: the person literally cannot feel fear.

08Cardiovascular System

Heart anatomy, blood flow, valves, and blood typing.

Complete blood flow

Starting with deoxygenated blood returning from the body, trace the complete path through both sides of the heart, naming every chamber and valve in order.

  1. Deoxygenated blood arrives via vena cavae → Right atrium
  2. → Tricuspid valve → Right ventricle
  3. → Pulmonary semilunar valve → Pulmonary artery → Lungs
  4. Gas exchange: CO₂ out, O₂ in → Pulmonary veins → Left atrium
  5. → Bicuspid (mitral) valve → Left ventricle
  6. → Aortic semilunar valve → Aorta → Body
Valve function

What is the general purpose of heart valves? Distinguish between the atrioventricular (AV) valves and the semilunar valves.

Valves prevent backflow, ensuring one-directional blood flow. AV valves (tricuspid on the right; bicuspid/mitral on the left) prevent backflow from ventricles into atria during ventricular contraction. Semilunar valves (pulmonary and aortic) prevent backflow from arteries into ventricles after ventricular contraction.
Backflow prevention

What specific valve prevents blood from flowing back into the right ventricle after it has been pumped out? What about the left ventricle?

Right ventricle → Pulmonary semilunar valve. Left ventricle → Aortic semilunar valve. Both are semilunar valves that close when ventricular pressure drops below arterial pressure.
Blood typing scenario

A Type O patient receives Type A blood. Describe in molecular terms what goes wrong.

Type O individuals have anti-A AND anti-B antibodies in their plasma. The donor's Type A RBCs carry A antigens. The recipient's anti-A antibodies bind those antigens → agglutination (clumping) → complement activation and hemolysis (RBC destruction) → potentially fatal transfusion reaction.
Anti-serum test

Anti-A serum is added to a blood sample and it agglutinates. Anti-B serum causes no reaction. What blood type is the sample?

The sample is Type A. It has A antigens (reacts with Anti-A) but no B antigens (no reaction with Anti-B). If both antisera caused agglutination it would be AB; if neither, it would be O.
Universal donor/recipient

Why is Type O considered the universal donor? Why is Type AB the universal plasma donor?

Type O: RBCs have no A or B antigens, so no recipient's antibodies can attack them. Type AB plasma: AB individuals have no anti-A or anti-B antibodies in their plasma, so their plasma won't attack any recipient's RBCs. These are different concepts — whole blood vs. plasma donation.
09Respiratory System

Breathing mechanics (pressure-volume relationships), lung volumes, gas exchange, and airway anatomy.

Inspiration mechanics

Explain why air flows into the lungs during inspiration using the relationship between volume and pressure.

Diaphragm and external intercostals contract → thoracic cavity volume increases → by Boyle's Law, lung pressure decreases below atmospheric → air flows in from high pressure (atmosphere) to low pressure (lungs). Inspiration is an active process requiring muscle work.
Expiration mechanics

Fill in the blanks: Muscle _______ causes a _______ in lung volume, which causes a _______ in lung pressure, which causes air to rush _______ (inspiration/expiration).

Muscle relaxation → decrease in lung volume → increase in lung pressure → air rushes out = expiration. At rest this is passive (no muscle contraction needed); during exercise, accessory muscles actively compress the thorax to force more air out.
Lung volumes

Define tidal volume, vital capacity, and residual volume. How do the last two relate to total lung capacity?

Tidal volume: air moved per normal breath (~500 mL). Vital capacity: maximum volume that can be forcibly exhaled after maximum inhalation. Residual volume: air remaining in lungs after maximum exhalation (keeps alveoli from collapsing). Total Lung Capacity = Vital Capacity + Residual Volume.
Gas exchange

How does O₂ move from alveoli into the blood, and how does CO₂ move the opposite way? Name the driving force and vessels involved.

Both gases move by diffusion along their partial pressure gradients — from high partial pressure to low. O₂: alveoli (high pO₂) → capillaries (low pO₂). CO₂: capillaries (high pCO₂) → alveoli (low pCO₂), then exhaled. The thin alveolar walls (one cell thick) and the dense capillary network maximize diffusion efficiency.
Airway protection

What structure prevents food and liquid from entering the trachea during swallowing? What happens if it malfunctions?

The epiglottis — a leaf-shaped flap of elastic cartilage at the larynx entrance. During swallowing, the larynx rises, pushing against the epiglottis, which folds down over the glottis (tracheal opening). Malfunction: food/liquid enters the airway → choking, or repeated small aspirations → aspiration pneumonia.
Diaphragm

The diaphragm is the primary breathing muscle. What tissue type is it, and how is its control unique compared to other skeletal muscles?

The diaphragm is skeletal muscle tissue. Uniquely, it is controlled both involuntarily (by the respiratory centers in the medulla oblongata and pons of the brainstem — you breathe even when unconscious) and voluntarily (you can consciously hold your breath or breathe deeply). Most skeletal muscle is purely voluntary.
Pathway of air

List the structures air passes through from the nose to the alveoli in order.

  1. Nasal cavity (filters, warms, humidifies)
  2. Pharynx (throat — shared with digestive system)
  3. Larynx (voice box; epiglottis guards entry)
  4. Trachea (windpipe; C-shaped cartilage rings)
  5. Primary bronchi (right and left)
  6. Secondary and tertiary bronchi
  7. Bronchioles (no cartilage; smooth muscle walls)
  8. Alveoli (gas exchange)
10Blood, Hemostasis & Immune System

Blood components, stopping bleeding, immunity types, fever, and pathogen classification.

Blood components

What is hemoglobin, and how does it differ from hematocrit and from erythrocytes?

Hemoglobin: iron-containing protein inside RBCs that binds O₂ (and some CO₂) — a molecule. Erythrocytes: the red blood cells themselves that contain hemoglobin — a cell. Hematocrit: a lab measurement — the percentage of total blood volume made up of RBCs (normal: ~45% male, ~40% female) — a ratio.
Hemostasis sequence

A knife cuts a blood vessel. Walk through all three stages of hemostasis in order, and identify which is temporary vs. permanent.

  1. Vascular spasm — damaged vessel constricts reflexively. Fastest but most temporary — lasts minutes.
  2. Platelet plug formation — platelets adhere, become activated, aggregate at the wound. Temporary mechanical seal.
  3. Coagulation — clotting factor cascade activates; fibrinogen → fibrin mesh traps RBCs → permanent stable clot.
Immunity comparison

Compare innate and adaptive immunity across three dimensions: specificity, speed of response, and immunological memory.

InnateAdaptive

Fever pathway

Trace the events from bacterial infection to fever, identifying the cells, the chemical messengers, and the specific brain structure involved.

  1. Bacteria enter the body.
  2. Macrophages (WBCs) detect them and release pyrogens (e.g., interleukin-1).
  3. Pyrogens travel via blood to the hypothalamus — the brain's thermostat.
  4. The hypothalamus raises the temperature set point.
  5. Body generates heat: shivering, vasoconstriction → fever. Fever speeds immune activity and inhibits many pathogens.
Classify pathogens

Classify each pathogen as bacterium, virus, fungus, protist, or worm: tetanus, athlete's foot, malaria, rabies, pinworms.

Tetanus → Bacterium. Athlete's foot → Fungus. Malaria → Protist (Plasmodium). Rabies → Virus. Pinworms → Worm (helminth). Treatment varies dramatically by type: antibiotics for bacteria, antivirals for viruses, antifungals for fungi, antiparasitics for protists/worms.
Innate defenses

Sort these into innate or adaptive immunity: skin as a barrier, antibody production, phagocytosis by neutrophils, fever, T-cell attack on infected cells, natural killer cells.

Innate: skin/surface barriers, phagocytosis by neutrophils, fever, natural killer (NK) cells. Adaptive: antibody production (B-cell/humoral response), T-cell (cytotoxic) attack on infected cells. NK cells are innate because they don't need to recognize specific antigens — they attack any cell displaying stress markers.
Clinical thinking

A patient takes a medication that blocks platelet aggregation (like aspirin). Which step of hemostasis is most affected, and what is the clinical risk?

The platelet plug formation step is impaired. Clinical risk: minor cuts bleed longer than normal because the temporary seal can't form effectively. This is intentionally used therapeutically to prevent unwanted clots (strokes, heart attacks) — but creates risk of excessive bleeding with injury or surgery.
